# Market Intelligence Methodology
## What makes a competitive analysis report worth 100,000–500,000 SEK

**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Purpose:** Reference methodology for building premium market intelligence deliverables

---

## The Core Separation

A free blog post **describes** competitors. A premium consulting report **changes decisions**.

The price difference is not about length or hours spent. It is about the gap between:
- **Competitive observation** — anyone can read websites
- **Competitive intelligence** — structured research surfacing what is not publicly visible, interpreted through a decision-relevant framework, calibrated to one client's specific strategic situation

---

## Part 1: Analytical Frameworks

Premium competitive analysis layers multiple frameworks — each answers a different question.

### Porter's Five Forces
**Answers:** Why is this industry more or less profitable? Where is power concentrated?

Five forces to score with evidence:
1. Threat of new entrants — barriers to entry
2. Threat of substitutes — what else buyers can use
3. Supplier bargaining power — who controls input costs
4. Buyer bargaining power — how much pricing power customers hold
5. Rivalry intensity — price wars, differentiation battles, capacity dynamics

**Premium output:** Each force scored (1–5) and narrative-supported with named evidence. Verdict: industry attractiveness for investment, entry, or defense.

---

### Blue Ocean / Strategy Canvas
**Answers:** Where is competitive whitespace? Where is everyone fighting unnecessarily?

Map all competing factors (price, service, features, speed, etc.) on a canvas — plot every competitor's investment across these factors. Whitespace = factor combinations that create new demand rather than fighting over existing demand.

**Premium output:** Visual strategy canvas + recommended "eliminate-reduce-raise-create" grid.

---

### SWOT — Cross-Referenced, Not Listed
**Answers:** What strategic options does our position generate?

Premium SWOT cross-references cells:
- Strengths × Opportunities = leverage plays
- Strengths × Threats = defense plays
- Weaknesses × Opportunities = build/buy/partner decision
- Weaknesses × Threats = existential risks

**Premium output:** 2×2 strategic options matrix with named actions, not a bullet list.

---

### GE/McKinsey Nine-Box Matrix
**Answers:** Across segments or product lines, where should we invest vs. harvest?

Two composite axes:
- **Industry Attractiveness:** market size, growth, profitability, competitive intensity, cyclicality
- **Business Strength:** relative share, margin vs. industry, competitive capability, brand

More nuanced than BCG because both axes are multi-factor composites, not proxies.

---

### BCG Growth-Share Matrix
**Answers:** How should we allocate resources across a portfolio?

Four quadrants — Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs — require calculating *relative* market share (vs. largest competitor), not absolute share.

---

### Value Chain Analysis
**Answers:** Where does a competitor build advantage? Where are their cost vulnerabilities?

Primary activities: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing/sales, service.
Support activities: infrastructure, HR, technology, procurement.

**Premium output:** Side-by-side value chain comparison identifying structural cost gaps and differentiation leverage points.

---

### PESTEL
**Answers:** What macro forces are changing competitive dynamics?

Political, Economic, Sociocultural, Technological, Environmental, Legal — each populated with specific sourced evidence. Generic observations are not premium; specific regulatory changes linked to named competitive implications are.

---

### War Gaming / Scenario Analysis
**Answers:** How will competitors respond to our moves?

McKinsey approach: teams role-play as specific competitors using fact packs (financials, decision-makers, strategic posture). Simulate moves and counter-moves across rounds.

**Premium output:** Converts competitive analysis from a static snapshot into a dynamic decision playbook.

## Part 2: Standard Report Structure

Governed by the **Pyramid Principle**: conclusion first, supporting arguments second, evidence third. Decision-makers read executive summaries; they do not start with appendices.

### 1. Executive Summary (2–4 pages)
- The "so what" in the first paragraph
- 3–5 key findings
- Recommended strategic actions with owners and timeframes
- Cost of inaction stated explicitly
- Written last, reads first

### 2. Scope and Methodology
- Research questions this report answers
- Data sources used (primary vs. secondary, with source names)
- Timeframe of data collection
- Limitations and confidence levels
- Competitor set definition and rationale

### 3. Market Context and Sizing
- TAM/SAM/SOM — dual methodology (top-down + bottom-up cross-validated)
- Market growth drivers and headwinds
- Segment definition and sizing
- Geographic breakdown
- CAGR projections sourced and triangulated

### 4. Competitive Landscape Overview
- Market structure visualization (fragmented vs. concentrated)
- Market share table with growth trends
- Positioning map (perceptual map) — competitors plotted on 2 axes most relevant to client
- Category groupings (Tier 1/2/3 or direct/adjacent/potential entrants)

### 5. Individual Competitor Profiles
For each significant competitor:
- Business model and revenue structure
- Financial performance (revenue, growth, margin, funding/ownership)
- Product/service portfolio with differentiators
- Pricing model and price points
- Target customer profile and acquisition approach
- Geographic presence and expansion trajectory
- Organizational size, key leadership, talent strategy
- Strategic priorities (signals from earnings calls, job postings, PR, roadmap)
- Identified vulnerabilities and areas of investment

### 6. Competitive Dynamics Analysis
- Porter's Five Forces assessment (scored with evidence)
- Win/loss analysis: where client wins vs. loses, and against whom
- Battlecard-level competitive insights
- Recent competitive moves (price changes, launches, M&A, key hires)

### 7. Pricing Analysis
- Pricing model matrix across all competitors
- Price point comparison by tier/package
- Value-for-money positioning map
- Pricing strategy classification (cost-plus / value-based / competitive / penetration)
- Price elasticity signals (from churn data, testimonials, customer interviews)

### 8. Market Sizing — Methodology Detail
- Full methodology disclosure
- Top-down calculation with source chain
- Bottom-up calculation (customer count × ARPU)
- Cross-validation and reconciliation
- Sensitivity analysis (bear/base/bull case)

### 9. Strategic Implications
- "Where to play" — segments, geographies, customer profiles
- "How to win" — capabilities to build, buy, or partner
- Whitespace opportunities identified
- Threats requiring defensive action and timelines
- Recommended positioning relative to named competitors

### 10. Decision Framework / Recommendations
- Prioritized action list (3–5, not 20)
- Decision criteria: if X then do A, if Y then do B
- Resource requirements per recommendation
- Success metrics and review triggers

### Appendix
- Full data tables
- Interview protocol and respondent profiles (anonymized)
- Methodology detail
- Source list

## Part 3: Data Sources

This is the largest single differentiator between a blog post and a paid report.

### Secondary Research (Desk Research)
Available to anyone — but premium reports use paid/gated sources:

- Annual reports, 10-K/10-Q filings, investor presentations
- Patent filings (technology direction signals)
- Job postings (reveals capability build-out and strategic priorities)
- Press releases and executive interview archives
- Paid databases: Gartner, Forrester, IDC, Euromonitor, Mintel, Bloomberg, Capital IQ
- Trade publications and sector journals
- Court filings, regulatory submissions (often ignored, highly revealing)
- LinkedIn: company data, hiring velocity
- SimilarWeb / SEMrush / Ahrefs: digital footprint analysis
- Glassdoor: organizational health signals
- Product review platforms: G2, Capterra, Trustpilot (voice of competitor's customer)

### Primary Research — What Actually Justifies the Price

This is what cannot be Googled:

| Method | What It Surfaces |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Expert network interviews** (GLG, Guidepoint, AlphaSights) | Former competitor executives — strategic intent, internal dynamics, roadmap signals |
| **Customer win/loss interviews** | Actual decision criteria, not assumed ones; why buyers chose or rejected competitors |
| **Mystery shopping** | Competitor pricing, sales methodology, discount structures, contract terms, sales cycle length |
| **Channel partner interviews** | Resellers/distributors who work with multiple competitors simultaneously — direct comparison |
| **Quantitative surveys** | Buyer preference, brand perception, NPS benchmarking across competitive set |
| **Observation research** | Trade shows, conferences, public demos, product walkthroughs |

**The ratio of primary to secondary research determines the price:**
- 100% secondary → defensible at 50,000–80,000 SEK
- 20+ expert interviews + 50+ customer win/loss calls → justifies 300,000–500,000 SEK

## Part 4: Market Sizing Methodology

### Top-Down Approach
1. Start with a published total market figure (Gartner, Forrester, government statistics, trade association)
2. Apply segmentation filters: geography, customer type, use case
3. Derive TAM → SAM → SOM through sequential narrowing
4. Source every filter applied with rationale

**Example:**
Global CRM market = $70B (IDC 2025)
→ Enterprise segment = 38% = $26.6B
→ Nordic geography = 2.1% = $560M TAM
→ Client's product tier addressable = 15% = $84M SAM

### Bottom-Up Approach
1. Define ideal customer profile precisely
2. Count qualifying companies (Bisnode, D&B, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, business registries)
3. Estimate average annual contract value (from pricing analysis, public case studies, proxy data)
4. Multiply: TAM = N × ACV
5. Apply realistic conversion estimates for SOM

**Example:**
8,400 qualifying companies in Sweden × 95,000 SEK ACV = 798M SEK TAM

### Cross-Validation Standard
- If top-down and bottom-up fall within 15% of each other → estimate is robust
- If divergence > 15% → re-examine assumptions
- Premium reports show both calculations, explain divergence, present three scenarios

### Sensitivity Analysis
One-way sensitivity table showing how SAM changes if ACV ±20% or addressable segment ±10%. Demonstrates analytical rigor and builds client trust.

## Part 5: Competitive Pricing Analysis

### Step 1: Price Discovery
- Public pricing pages
- Sales team intelligence from internal win/loss debrief
- Mystery shopping through competitor sales cycles
- Customer interviews (what did they pay, what discounts were offered?)
- Job postings (often mention ACV targets and pricing tier structures)
- Analyst reports and industry publications

### Step 2: Pricing Model Classification Matrix
Map all competitors across:
- **Model:** subscription / usage-based / per-seat / one-time / hybrid
- **Anchoring:** low-end entry vs. premium-first
- **Tier structure:** number of tiers, what gates each tier
- **Bundling logic:** included vs. add-on
- **Discount patterns:** volume, annual commitment, competitive displacement
- **Free trial / freemium:** yes/no, length, conversion rate (where estimable)

### Step 3: Price-Value Positioning Map
Plot competitors on two axes:
- X: relative price (low → high)
- Y: relative perceived value (feature count, ratings, brand strength)

Identifies:
- **Overpriced:** high price, low value → vulnerable to displacement
- **Underpriced:** high value, low price → potential market disruptors
- **Premium players:** what justifies the premium?
- **Value tier:** what are they sacrificing to compete on price?

### Step 4: Feature-Price Matrix
Cross-tabulate features against price tiers across all competitors:
- **Table stakes:** universal features (not a differentiator)
- **Premium features:** features that command price premiums
- **Whitespace:** features missing from the market entirely
- **"Good enough" tier:** where most buyers actually purchase

### Step 5: Pricing Strategy Inference
- **Cost-plus:** margin-driven, signals commoditization
- **Value-based:** price set by buyer's willingness to pay
- **Competitive:** shadow pricing against the market leader
- **Penetration:** below-market entry to gain share (typically temporary)
- **Skimming:** high initial price for early adopters, erodes over time

### Step 6: Implications and Recommendations
- Where is there room to charge more?
- Where is a competitor structurally vulnerable on price?
- What pricing model shift would change competitive dynamics?
- What feature investments would unlock a price tier upgrade?

## Part 6: The 10 Value Drivers That Justify Premium Pricing

### 1. Specificity
A blog post is written for 10,000 readers. A premium report is written for one decision-maker in one company facing one specific competitive situation. Every section, every competitor selected, every recommendation is calibrated to a named client's context. This cannot be reproduced at scale.

### 2. Primary Research Access
The central value driver. Expert network interviews, mystery shopping, win/loss calls — these produce information that does not exist publicly. Clients pay for the intelligence, not the hours.

### 3. Analyst Interpretation
A premium analyst knows which signals matter: six senior engineering hires in a new stack = product pivot 12–18 months out; Glassdoor score drop + exec departures = organizational instability; pricing simplification = preparation for market expansion. Interpretation requires domain expertise that cannot be templated.

### 4. Decision Framework Output
Blog posts describe. Premium reports prescribe. The deliverable connects analysis to specific decisions with named competitors, timelines, and resource estimates. Outcome-oriented, not information delivery.

### 5. Structured Methodology
When a CFO asks "how did you arrive at this market size?", a premium report has a defensible, auditable answer: dual methodology + named sources + sensitivity analysis. Defensibility under scrutiny is risk reduction for the client. That has measurable value.

### 6. Comparative Intelligence
Generic frameworks describe how industries work. Premium reports answer: "Compared to Competitor A's pricing structure, your current positioning has three specific vulnerabilities, and here is how to address them." Requires knowing both the client's internal reality and the external competitive reality simultaneously.

### 7. Confidentiality and Non-Replication
The client is paying, in part, for the fact that the same analysis will not be sold to their competitor. In competitive markets, this exclusivity has direct economic value.

### 8. Credibility as Decision Cover
Decisions at the 50M–500M SEK level require organizational alignment. An authoritative, rigorous report from a credible firm provides decision cover — it allows leaders to move forward with confidence and defend choices to boards, investors, or regulators. The report is not just information; it is organizational permission to act.

### 9. Speed and Compression
A capable consulting team with established expert networks and research infrastructure can produce in 6–8 weeks what would take an in-house team 6–12 months — and with higher accuracy because of access to sources the client cannot reach. Time compression is measurable value.

### 10. War Gaming and Forward Planning
Premium deliverables include forward-looking scenario work: "If Competitor B launches a freemium tier in Q3, here are the three most likely moves and how you should respond to each." This converts the report from a historical snapshot into a decision playbook.

## Summary: Blog Post vs. 200,000 SEK Report

| Dimension | Free Blog Post | Premium Report |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Research sources | Public web | Expert networks, mystery shopping, win/loss calls, paid databases |
| Competitor coverage | Generic descriptions | Named, sourced, financial-grade profiles |
| Market sizing | Cited from one report | Dual-method TAM/SAM/SOM with sensitivity analysis |
| Frameworks applied | Named but not applied | Applied to client's actual situation with scored outputs |
| Pricing analysis | Mentions pricing exists | Feature-price matrix, positioning map, strategy inference |
| Strategic implications | Generic "differentiate" | Specific moves, named competitors, timelines, resource estimates |
| Decision framework | None | If/then decision logic tied to business outcomes |
| Defensibility | Cannot be audited | Methodology section, source list, confidence levels stated |
| Specificity | Applies to everyone | Written for one client, one market, one decision |
| Output format | Article | Pyramid-structured deck + appendix + data tables |
